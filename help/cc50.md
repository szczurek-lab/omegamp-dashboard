## CC50 -- Cytotoxicity

CC50 is the concentration at which a peptide reduces the viability of mammalian cells by 50%, measured against HEK293T cells (human embryonic kidney). It quantifies the peptide's toxicity to nucleated human cells and is a key safety metric for therapeutic development.

### Assay method

Cell viability is assessed after peptide exposure across a range of concentrations. The CC50 is determined by non-linear regression of the dose-response curve considering all replicate measurements. Lower CC50 values indicate greater cytotoxicity (more toxic); higher values indicate a safer peptide.

### Right-censoring at >128 uM

For peptides where cytotoxicity remained well below 50% across the tested concentration range, the CC50 would need to be extrapolated beyond the experimental data by extending the non-linear regression curve. Following standard practice in AMP literature, these values are right-censored and reported as >128 uM rather than relying on extrapolated estimates.

### Safety Window (SW)

The Safety Window is calculated as CC50 divided by MIC. Higher SW values indicate a wider margin between the antimicrobially effective concentration and the cytotoxic concentration.

### CC50 vs HC50

CC50 and HC50 measure toxicity through different pathways. Erythrocytes (HC50) are pure membrane targets since they lack nuclei and organelles, so HC50 is a clean readout of membrane-lytic toxicity. HEK293T cells (CC50) can die through additional mechanisms including apoptosis, mitochondrial disruption, and intracellular target interference. Consequently, CC50 and HC50 do not always track together, and a peptide can be hemolytic without being cytotoxic or vice versa.
