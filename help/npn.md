## NPN Uptake -- Outer Membrane Permeabilization

The NPN (1-N-phenylnaphthylamine) uptake assay measures outer membrane integrity. NPN is a hydrophobic fluorescent probe that fluoresces weakly in aqueous solution but strongly in hydrophobic environments. An intact outer membrane excludes NPN; when a peptide disrupts the outer membrane, NPN partitions into the exposed lipid bilayer, producing a fluorescence increase.

### Gram-negative vs. Gram-positive context

This assay is specific to Gram-negative bacteria, which possess a distinctive outer membrane composed of lipopolysaccharide (LPS) on its external leaflet and phospholipids on its inner leaflet. This outer membrane acts as a permeability barrier that AMPs must breach before reaching the cytoplasmic membrane. Gram-positive bacteria lack this outer membrane entirely; their cell envelope consists of a thick peptidoglycan layer surrounding a single cytoplasmic membrane. NPN uptake is therefore not applicable to Gram-positive species and is measured here exclusively against the Gram-negative indicator strain.

### Experimental conditions

All measurements were performed against *A. baumannii* ATCC 19606 at each peptide's MIC. Triton X-100 (complete membrane lysis) serves as the positive control; Polymyxin B and Levofloxacin are included as antibiotic references.

### Reported metrics

- **MaxRel (%)**: Maximum fluorescence intensity relative to the untreated baseline. Higher values indicate greater outer membrane disruption at peak response.
- **AUC**: Area under the fluorescence-time curve (integrated response). Captures both intensity and duration of membrane disruption.

### Reading the scatter plot

Peptides in the **upper-right quadrant** (high MaxRel, high AUC) are strong permeabilizers with sustained outer membrane disruption. **Upper-left** (low MaxRel, high AUC) are slow responders. **Lower-right** (high MaxRel, low AUC) are transient responders with a brief burst of permeabilization. Dashed lines mark the median across all tested peptides.

### Mechanistic context

NPN-dominant peptides (high NPN, low DiSC3(5)) primarily disrupt the Gram-negative outer membrane without strongly depolarizing the cytoplasmic membrane. This mechanistic profile is associated with substantially lower hemolytic toxicity compared to DiSC-dominant peptides, likely because the outer membrane disruption mechanism does not translate to eukaryotic cells, which lack an LPS-containing outer membrane.
