## HC50 -- Hemolysis

HC50 is the concentration at which a peptide lyses 50% of human red blood cells (erythrocytes). It is the primary safety metric for assessing membrane-lytic toxicity, since erythrocytes are pure membrane targets with no nucleus, organelles, or active repair mechanisms.

### Assay method

HC50 is determined by non-linear regression of the dose-response curve from red blood cell lysis measurements after exposure to the peptides across a range of concentrations, considering all replicate results.

### Why hemolysis matters

Cationic antimicrobial peptides kill bacteria by disrupting their membranes. However, if a peptide also disrupts eukaryotic membranes, it will lyse red blood cells upon intravenous administration, causing hemolytic anemia. HC50 directly measures this risk.

### Right-censoring at >128 uM

For peptides where hemolysis remained well below 50% across the tested concentration range, the HC50 would need to be extrapolated beyond the experimental data by extending the non-linear regression curve. Following standard practice in AMP literature, these values are right-censored and reported as >128 uM rather than relying on extrapolated estimates.

### Safety Window (SW)

The hemolytic Safety Window (SW_HC50) is calculated as HC50 divided by MIC. Because HC50 specifically measures membrane toxicity, SW_HC50 is the more mechanistically informative safety metric for membranolytic AMPs.
